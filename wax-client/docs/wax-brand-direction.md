# WAX Brand Direction

## Positioning

WAX is not a classic fashion house and it should not feel like a generic ecommerce storefront.

WAX should feel like a contemporary atelier for bespoke 3D pieces.

Core idea:

`Luxury editorial + sculptural depth + bespoke 3D creation`

## Official Reference Signals

This direction is informed by official brand cues observed from luxury maisons such as Goyard and the public-facing presence of Louis Vuitton.

Signals worth learning from:

- calm, high-contrast hierarchy
- editorial image-led layouts
- heritage and craft language
- product as cultural object, not commodity
- restrained navigation and generous spacing

Signals WAX should avoid copying directly:

- legacy European maison codes as-is
- exact palettes, pattern systems, or monogram-like cues
- overt heritage mimicry
- static catalog-only storytelling

## WAX Personality

WAX should feel:

- sculptural
- precise
- immersive
- bespoke
- luxurious without being aristocratic
- technological without feeling like a startup

## Visual Thesis

WAX presents custom 3D pieces as collectible objects.

The interface should communicate:

- object first
- process matters
- customization is premium
- volume, light, and surface are central

## Creative Pillars

### 1. Object Before Interface

The product or concept image should dominate the composition.

UI should frame the object, not compete with it.

### 2. Sculptural Editorial

Use the discipline of luxury editorial design, but add a sense of mass, depth, and dimensionality.

### 3. Bespoke Creation

Customization is not a feature badge. It is the center of the brand promise.

Language should emphasize commission, shaping, refinement, and final form.

### 4. Curated Technology

Technology should feel invisible, refined, and intentional.

Avoid visual cues that feel overly SaaS, futuristic, or cold.

### 5. Material Warmth

The visual system should feel tactile: wax, resin, leather, stone, metal, powder, light.

## Palette Direction

### Primary neutrals

- Bone
- Porcelain
- Ink
- Graphite

### Warm accents

- Wax Amber
- Oxblood
- Bronze

### Support tones

- Smoke
- Stone
- Deep Olive

The palette should stay restrained. Accent colors should be rare and deliberate.

## Typography Direction

Use contrast between:

- editorial serif for statements, section titles, and hero typography
- refined sans for UI, metadata, labels, and longer body text

Recommended tone, not mandatory implementation:

- serif: elegant, sharp, cultivated
- sans: quiet, modern, precise

## Layout Principles

- generous white space
- asymmetric balance, not chaotic collage
- long vertical rhythm
- image-led storytelling
- product cards should feel curated, not marketplace-like

## Motion Principles

- slow fades
- controlled reveals
- minimal hover shifts
- no playful bounce
- no aggressive parallax

Motion should feel like unveiling, not animation for its own sake.

## Imagery Direction

Preferred imagery qualities:

- close-ups on surfaces and edges
- sharp silhouettes
- shadow depth
- calm backgrounds
- premium object isolation

When process is shown, it should feel ceremonial and crafted, not mechanical.

## Verbal Direction

Preferred words:

- piece
- bespoke
- sculpted
- refined
- atelier
- commission
- form
- finish
- crafted
- detail

Use cautiously:

- AI
- generator
- automation
- instant
- workflow

The product story should foreground creative authorship and refinement, not raw tooling.

## UX Implications

WAX should feel more like an atelier experience than a conventional store.

This means:

- fewer loud CTAs
- quieter but stronger hierarchy
- more narrative blocks
- more trust and craft cues
- more emphasis on personalization paths

## First Screens Priority

The first screens should communicate:

1. WAX creates bespoke 3D pieces
2. the pieces feel collectible and premium
3. customization is central
4. the brand is contemporary, not traditionalist

## Design Check

Before approving a screen, ask:

1. Does it feel premium?
2. Does it feel sculptural?
3. Does it foreground bespoke creation?
4. Does it feel more atelier than marketplace?
5. Does the technology feel refined rather than loud?
